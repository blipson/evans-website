Everybody's Looking for the Meaning of Life Through a Cell Phone Screen...

  It seems like I can't escape the often-repeated phrase, "Don’t get caught up in social media because it is curated snapshots that give the illusion of what someone’s life is like". On Facebook, Instagram, or Twitter, I still have these involuntary urges of the very well named FOMO(Feeling of missing out). I get these feelings as if I am just wasting my life doing nothing while the accounts I follow keep posting pictures of their mind-blowing salad or the amazing time they had in (insert club/concert/foreign country). Honestly, the amount of time I have spent thinking about how I should act and post on social media platforms is both sad and exhausting. Is it a symptom of being in my twenties that I feel this way? Why does the constant stream of “Show and Tell” feel so accomplishing and degrading simultaneously?

  I feel sometimes stuck in this world of supposed “global connection”. My mind gets caught between two very opposing social pressures:

  • You must use social media platforms because you will miss out on/not be invited to what your friends, family, role models, musicians, actors etc… are doing. Having the ability to be connected with friends and family around the world is an evolution of our already social minds. “A lot of good comes out of staying connected!”
  • Social media is a toxic sinkhole of isolation versus connection, a window of false-realities that usually leads to feelings of envy and the distorted beliefs that others lead happier lives than you. “Having a healthy relationship with social media is important!”

  I feel like a lot of us at one point have jumped onto Facebook and saw a group of friends went out last night, and this feeling of isolation and resentment comes rushing in. Would you have been invited if you were online more often? At least on my end, a lot of the time my mind starts racing for excuses or justifications as to why I was not invited or missed out on something. “It probably wouldn’t have been that fun anyway.” “I wasn’t really interested in going out last night.” Something always seems to get to me though. This feeling of envy that my friends are always going on fun adventures, capturing the moments that mean so much to them, sinks in. “Do my friends not want to spend time with me?” “I bet they did not have as much fun because I wasn’t there.” Why do I have so much trouble with acceptance that so much of social media, especially sites like Instagram and SnapChat, are so blatantly fraudulent? I very clearly know this and understand this concept, but it’s like I cannot accept this truth. It feels like subconsciously there is a part of me saying “That can’t be my world too. My world is one hundred percent genuine. Why would anyone want to fool me?”

  The worst feeling about all of this is that I somehow end up with more frustrations than resolutions every time I get consciously caught up in these thoughts. Sometimes I want to get onto Facebook and post pictures that I have taken and share my thoughts, music, videos, whatever, and often I end up talking myself out of it one way or another. I usually end up agreeing that no one would care what I post. I barely post anyway, especially on Facebook. “Do I even show up on people’s feeds anymore?” Since Facebook does personalized feeds how many of the friends that I would want to reach would actually see the post? “My life is boring anyway.” “I don’t do near as cool stuff as my friends.” “God, their lives are so cool.” “I wish my life could be like this.”

  I feel like some readers may be looking at this and immediately come to the conclusion that I am spending way too much time stressing about the stress of social media and that I should just let it go. If it is such a problem, then I should just get off of it all and call it good. That thought has lead me to the biggest question of all of this: Why even bother with Facebook, Instagram and all the aforementioned platforms? Simply put, I do like it. I think it is an incredible thing that I can be able to with a few clicks or taps on my phone, watch someone’s live stream in Korea on Facebook, or I can make connections with fans of my favorite musicians and have a feeling of community when it comes to fan followings. There is just something toxic about social media these days. Maybe it boils down to that I just happen to be in the demographic that almost all social media platforms market to. I am the person that these brands want to be using their applications day in and day out. I just can’t escape the feeling that social media is a black hole of narcissism and self-validation, that feeds off of itself, which then continues to breed narcissism and self-validation.

  I guess I don’t really have some perfectly summed up moral of this whole story. Never have I put thoughts like these into readable words before and I think that was the most freeing of this all. Sometimes I feel like I am the only person who thinks the thoughts that I have. But while my thoughts will always be unique to me, I have to believe that someone else has at least contemplated these ideas before. I think at the beginning of writing this all down, I did want to have some kind of enlightened, wholesome wrap-up of my thoughts that I could end with to make people think. I think that this really was for me the whole time. I need to connect with myself more. My fears about social media actually have nothing to do with me, and all have to do with how I think people will react to what I share and honestly, that’s just not fair at all to myself. I need to take time to appreciate the mind-blowing salad I had, or when I capture moments that mean so much to me, I need to not immediately start thinking about how I can put it on Facebook. I have gone back and forth many times on if I even want to put this online or if it should just stay a shelved diary type entry. Sometimes I feel like I have to post things online because I am expected to and if I was not active on social media, my friends would treat me differently. I want to show the world what I am interested in, and that’s the point of social media right? The difference is exactly what I said. I am supposed to show the world what I am interested in, not show the world what I think it wants from me.

  I will end this with a couple good quotes that I think really helped me get some of this out of my head and onto a computer. Allison Abrams in said in an article of hers (Written March fifth, twenty-seventeen): “With all of the suffering and pain in the world, wouldn’t it be a tragedy if people stopped sharing joyful events for fear of making someone else jealous? Imagine if people only discussed all of the negative things that surrounded them. Especially over this past year, don’t we have enough tragic posts appearing in our newsfeeds twenty-four-seven? Given that there will always be someone who’s taller, richer, better-looking, who has more friends, a better job, etc., we can either allow ourselves to fall into the dangerous trap of comparison, or we can choose to remember that regardless of what others around you appear to have, everyone is grappling with their own struggles. For every promotion, book deal and Tony nomination, chances are, the recipient has experienced equally or more significant life setbacks.” A Quote from Eckhart Tolle: “Most humans are never fully present in the now, because unconsciously they believe that the next moment must be more important than this one. But then you miss your whole life, which is never not now.”
